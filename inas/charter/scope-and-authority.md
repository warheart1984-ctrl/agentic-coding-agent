# Scope and Authority

## Scope

INAS defines the constitutional rules governing:

- **Evidence** — what counts as evidence, how it is structured, validated, inherited, and replayed
- **Assurance** — levels of constitutional fidelity, proofs, invariants, and failure modes
- **Conformance** — required, optional, and forbidden behaviors for conforming runtimes
- **Interoperability** — cross-runtime message formats, intent language, inference and continuity contracts
- **Lineage and Provenance** — state records, state engines, lineage guarantees
- **Constitutional Context** — the Constitutional Context Record (CCR) as the universal cross-runtime message
- **Federated Governance** — cross-runtime evidence exchange, validation, lineage verification, assurance proofs
- **Cross-Runtime Behavior** — how independent runtimes coordinate under constitutional rules

INAS applies to any system claiming conformance to constitutional computing principles, including but not limited to: CIEMS, CEIP (reference runtime), Nova, OpenAI build systems, sovereign OS runtimes, federated intelligence systems, and multi-world governed intelligence environments.

INAS does **not** prescribe implementation details, programming languages, runtime architectures, or deployment models.

## Authority

INAS derives its authority from the Constitution of Constitutional Computing and serves as the binding contract between:

- Constitutional intent
- Constitutional evidence
- Constitutional execution
- Constitutional validation

INAS is the **sole authoritative specification** for assurance and conformance. No runtime may override, reinterpret, or redefine INAS rules.

## Neutrality

INAS is:

- Vendor-neutral
- Runtime-neutral
- Architecture-neutral
- Cloud-neutral
- Model-neutral

It defines **what must be true**, not how it must be implemented.
